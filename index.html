Site Objectives

  • Understand key Supreme Court rulings affecting African Americans.
  • Analyze how legal reasoning changed over time.
  • Connect historical decisions with modern civil rights issues.

Welcome

Throughout American history, Supreme Court decisions have played a pivotal role in both advancing and hindering the civil rights of African Americans; landmark cases such as Plessy v. Ferguson, Brown v. Board of Education, and Loving v. Virginia reflect the evolving interpretation of constitutional principles and illustrate how the judiciary has been both a tool of systemic oppression and a catalyst for racial justice.This project explores how landmark Supreme Court decisions have shaped the legal, social, and political lives of African Americans from the 19th century to today. Through an interactive timeline and case studies, users will uncover the evolving role of the judiciary in civil rights.

Case Studies: Landmark Supreme Court Cases and the African American Experience

Explore how the Supreme Court has shaped — and often challenged — the rights of African Americans. Each case reflects a key turning point in the pursuit of justice and equality in America.

  1. Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857)

    Summary:

    The Court ruled that African Americans, whether enslaved or free, could not be U.S. citizens and had no right to sue in federal court. It also declared the Missouri Compromise unconstitutional, reinforcing slavery’s expansion.

    Decision:

    7–2 against Dred Scott

    Significance:

    This case denied Black Americans any legal standing and inflamed tensions that led to the Civil War. It remains one of the most infamously racist rulings in U.S. legal history.

  2. Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)

    Summary:

    The Court upheld a Louisiana law requiring separate railway cars for Black and white passengers, introducing the doctrine of “separate but equal” that justified racial segregation for over half a century.

    Decision:

    7–1 in favor of segregation

    Significance:

    Legitimized Jim Crow laws across the South and institutionalized racial discrimination in public life until the civil rights movement of the 1960s.

  3. Brown v. Board of Education (1954)

    Summary:

    The Court unanimously overturned Plessy, declaring that separate educational facilities for Black and white students are inherently unequal and violate the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment.

    Decision:

    9–0 in favor of desegregation

    Significance:

    A watershed moment for the civil rights movement, this ruling ignited nationwide efforts to desegregate schools and challenge systemic racism.

  4. Loving v. Virginia (1967)

    Summary:

    The Court struck down Virginia’s ban on interracial marriage, finding that such laws violated both the Equal Protection and Due Process Clauses of the 14th Amendment.

    Decision:

    9–0 in favor of the Lovings

    Significance:

    Affirmed the constitutional right to marry across racial lines and set a key precedent for later marriage equality cases.

  5. Regents of the University of California v. Bakke (1978)

    Summary:

    The Court ruled that explicit racial quotas in college admissions violate the Equal Protection Clause but allowed race to be considered as one factor in admissions decisions.

    Decision:

    Split decision — upheld Bakke’s claim but supported limited use of affirmative action

    Significance:

    Introduced legal boundaries for affirmative action policies and sparked ongoing debates about race and fairness in education.

  6. Grutter v. Bollinger (2003)

    Summary:

    The Court upheld the University of Michigan Law School’s policy of considering race as one of many factors in admissions, supporting diversity as a compelling state interest.

    Decision:

    5–4 in favor of the university

    Significance:

    Reinforced affirmative action policies and validated efforts to create diverse educational environments, but set the stage for future legal challenges.

  7. Shelby County v. Holder (2013)

    Summary:

    The Court struck down the coverage formula in the Voting Rights Act that required jurisdictions with histories of voter discrimination to seek federal approval before changing voting laws.

    Decision:

    5–4 against preclearance

    Significance:

    Gutted a core protection of the Voting Rights Act, enabling states to pass restrictive voting laws. Widely criticized for increasing voter suppression risks.
